# Image guide

Every placeholder below is a labeled `.svg` so the site already looks
complete. Replace each one with a real photo at roughly the same dimensions
and aspect ratio so the layout doesn't shift.

[Likely] One thing worth deciding deliberately, not by default: a lot of
generic stock photography of "traditional healers" in South Africa leans
into costume and performance for a tourist/outsider audience — bones laid
out dramatically, exaggerated expressions, staged "ritual" shots. That reads
as caricature, not as a professional's actual practice, and it tends to
undercut the "trust and respect" goal stated in the brief. The better
reference point is how an established professional service presents
itself — a therapist's site, a consultant's site — calm, direct, dignified.

## If you need to shoot these yourself

- Phone camera is fine at these resolutions. Shoot in daylight near a
  window, avoid direct flash.
- For product shots: same background (a plain cloth, board, or wall) for
  every single product — that consistency is what makes a shop grid look
  professional rather than thrown together.
- For portraits: ask Makhosi what she's comfortable showing. Some healers
  prefer not to show their face publicly — if that's the case here, a
  photo of hands preparing herbs, the consultation space itself, or
  significant objects (without a face) is a completely legitimate
  alternative for the hero/about slots. Tell me if that's the direction
  and I'll adjust the copy around it.

## Where to source images if you don't have your own yet

- Your own phone, today, is genuinely the best option — it's the real
  business, not a stand-in.
- If you need temporary stock photography to launch sooner: search stock
  libraries (e.g. Unsplash, Pexels) for "herbalist," "wellness consultation,"
  "dried herbs," "incense" rather than "sangoma" or "witch doctor" — the
  former returns calm, professional-context photography; the latter mostly
  returns the costumed/staged kind described above.
- Whatever you use, replace it with real photography of the actual practice
  and products as soon as you can — for a trust-based local service,
  authentic photos outperform stock every time a potential client compares
  options.
